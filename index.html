20220719
keeping pace

"lots of goals, little time"

I have around 26 major benchmarks that I need to meet within a very short period of time. That makes at least one benchmark every working day for the next month-and-some-change.

I come from a hands-on working background so adjusting to work-from-home has been a balancing act with productivity and burnout on one end of the scale and relaxing oblivion on the other.

Dedication to learning web design enough to produce regular blog content places weight on that balanced scale, weight that I need to make adjustments for.

I have a few practices that help in adjusting my work-life balance when I feel it's out of balance. The first practice is practice itself, how you approach practice effects outcomes of training and performance in a macro way. Building a good understanding of how you and others approach different habits, routines and practices is key for developing any skill.

The second practice is empathy. I need a break sometimes and being able to fully step away from things without guilt or anxiety is a crucial part of that work-life scale being balanced. I try to make empathy the cornerstone of behavior in my personal life and at work, which helps keep that scale balanced nicely.

The third and final practice is grit. I have learned through my military experience and general working life that all careers, jobs, and tasks have a spectrum of positive and negative moments available to be experienced while performing them. Grit is my ability to maintain the necessary state of being to perform well through the negative moments of the task, job, or career. I feel that I exercise grit when my "relaxing practice" takes over too much and I've procrastinated for too long or forgot a key detail and so must work through the mess it include it, being almost forced to put things into motion and provide an outcome. Reaching that threshold of too much work often happens to me when I become complacent about a task or responsibility, or when the work required of me is seemingly too much to handle.

Every time I approach that threshold does not result in exercising and raising the threshold, it's often scary and sometimes requires me to face-up to things I'm not prepared for, things like addressing my organization style or mental health. The biggest reason I even advocate for a set of practices or routines around work is because, whether we want to or not, as people we face that limit of work or willpower and it can be frightening, daunting - sometimes finding ourselves unable to push through the threshold. Not being able to break through the barriers we might fall back to our previous states, or worse for some who lack support or information. Having a solid foundation of practices is a support structure to stand on, a set of tools to adjust your balance. ..laiehu

jumping hurdles

"happy to be done with that project"

I finally finished the technical document project I started on Friday that featured a lovely technical document - challenging! I struggled with the over-sensative nature of the stick css property but overcame it with grace and beauty by frankensteining a lovely solution out of background colors!

My initial issue was that the menu would not stay in place and I was forced to shuffle around a bunch of the DOM and then restructure some of the css. After that sprint I had to make the text flow over the nav menu in the mobile version.

Though I would say that the page is not production ready, I also love that the solution to the menu never moving with the media query was a simple and literal gloss over the menu. I add body-matching background color to the body text so that it is still legible over the menu and you can still make menu selections through the dense column of reference text.

I'm looking forward to the day where I don't manually type every line of code out and can instead trust the sources of information or templating.
